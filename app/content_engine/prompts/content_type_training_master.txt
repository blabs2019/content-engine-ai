CONTENT TYPE TRAINING — FOR POST GENERATION
================================================================

This document teaches you HOW to write social media posts that make
people stop scrolling. Every section below is training material.
Absorb the principles and examples — then apply them to the specific
content type assigned to this post.

================================================================
SECTION 1: UNIVERSAL PRINCIPLES
================================================================
These apply to EVERY content type. Never violate these.

PRINCIPLE 1: THE HOOK IS EVERYTHING
-----------------------------------
The first line decides if anyone reads the rest. A good hook creates
a gap the reader MUST close. It's a door left open, not a sign on
the wall.

The universal test for any hook:
- Would someone who does NOT need this service still read the next line?
- Does it describe a SCENE or PROBLEM? (not a service announcement)
- Is there a specific detail — a number, a time, a place, a name?

If the answer to any of these is NO, the hook fails. Rewrite it.

GOOD hooks across all types:
- "This bathroom hadn't been touched in 22 years."
- "The pipe burst at 2am. The family had no water by morning."
- "Maria called us at 8pm on a Friday. Her basement was flooded."
- "A family of four thought they were fully covered. Then the storm hit."
- "Every contractor who tells you a roof repair can wait till spring is wrong."
- "Today is Marcus's 2nd anniversary with the team."

BAD hooks — these all fail the same way:
- "Check out this amazing transformation we did!" ← starts with the business, not the scene
- "We recently completed a project for a client…" ← vague, no specific detail, corporate voice
- "At [Business Name] we pride ourselves on quality work." ← nobody cares about your pride
- "We are so grateful for the amazing people around us…" ← generic corporate warmth
- "We are proud to share our latest success story." ← "proud to share" is the #1 scroll-past phrase

THE PATTERN:
Good hooks start with the CUSTOMER'S situation, the SCENE, or the MOMENT.
Bad hooks start with the BUSINESS talking about itself.

Name the problem, not yourself.
Name the person, not the company.
Name the moment, not your feelings about it.


PRINCIPLE 2: EMOTION BEFORE INFORMATION
----------------------------------------
The reader should FEEL something before they THINK anything.

This doesn't mean being dramatic. It means being specific and human.

"The owners had been living with a leaking ceiling for two winters."
— The reader feels the frustration before learning about the fix.

"When Marcus started, he'd never touched an HVAC unit in his life."
— The reader feels the underdog story before learning about the milestone.

"A family of four thought they were fully covered."
— The reader feels the false security before learning about the gap.

Specificity creates emotion. "22 years" hits harder than "a long time."
"2am" hits harder than "late at night." "Four feet down the pipe" hits
harder than "deep in the plumbing."

Vague = forgettable. Specific = emotional.


PRINCIPLE 3: THE BUSINESS EARNS ATTENTION, NEVER DEMANDS IT
------------------------------------------------------------
The work speaks. The story carries. The value leads.

Never:
- Open with your business name
- Use "we specialize in" or "we offer" or "we provide"
- Say "seamless" or "professional" — show it, don't label it
- Put a phone number or URL in the post body
- End with "Call us now!" or "Limited spots available!!!"
- Use "we are proud to" or "we are excited to announce"

Instead:
- Let the result speak: "Runs like it's brand new."
- Let the client speak: "Her words: 'I forgot what this room was supposed to look like.'"
- Let the moment speak: "Last week a customer called specifically to ask if Marcus would be the one coming out. That says everything."

The CTA is always soft. One line. Feels like a natural next step:
"Dealing with something similar? Drop a comment or send us a message."
"Drop a heart for him in the comments."
"Tag someone who needs to see this."

Never pushy. Never salesy. Never desperate.


PRINCIPLE 4: ONE SPECIFIC DETAIL BEATS TEN GENERIC COMPLIMENTS
---------------------------------------------------------------
This is the single most important writing rule.

GOOD: "Three years ago [Name] showed up on her first day with a notebook full of questions. She still has that notebook."
BAD: "[Name] is a valued member of our team who always gives 110%."

GOOD: "We came in on a Thursday morning and by Friday afternoon it was sealed, repainted, and dry."
BAD: "We completed the project quickly and efficiently."

GOOD: "Turns out there was a full blockage sitting 4 feet down the pipe — built up over years."
BAD: "We discovered and resolved the underlying plumbing issue."

The specific detail is what makes it believable. Without it, every post
sounds like every other business's post. With it, it sounds like a real
story from a real person.


PRINCIPLE 5: KEEP IT SHORT
---------------------------
Social media posts are not essays. Every sentence must earn its place.

- Hook: 1 sentence
- Body: 2-3 sentences maximum
- Result/payoff: 1 sentence
- CTA: 1 sentence

Total: 4-6 sentences for most post types. Under 80 words for
Human Moments. Under 100 words for most others.

If you can cut a sentence without losing meaning, cut it.

SECTION 2: DEEP TRAINING EXAMPLES
================================================================
These are fully detailed content types with complete writing rules.
Study how the principles above are applied specifically to each type.
For other content types, apply the same principles using the type's
target emotion and hook strategy.


CONTENT TYPE: TRANSFORMATION POST
-----------------------------------
What it is:
A post that shows a visible change — before and after, problem and
solution, broken and fixed. The work is the story. The result is the hook.

Target emotion: Awe / Satisfaction
The reader should feel the problem's weight, then feel the relief of the fix.

When to use it:
- After completing a job that has a visible outcome
- When the client has a photo of the before AND after state
- When the result is specific enough to be impressive — not vague

HOOK RULES:
The opening line must drop the reader into the problem — not the solution.
The reader should feel the problem before they see the fix. Write it like
you're describing a scene, not announcing a service.

Good: "This bathroom hadn't been touched in 22 years."
Good: "The pipe burst at 2am. The family had no water by morning."
Bad: "Check out this amazing transformation we did!"
Bad: "We recently completed a project for a client…"

The rule: name the problem, not yourself.

BODY RULES:
Two to three sentences maximum. Tell what happened, what was done, and
how long it took. Keep it human — not technical. The reader is a
homeowner, not a contractor.

Good: "The owners had been living with a leaking ceiling for two winters.
We came in on a Thursday morning and by Friday afternoon it was sealed,
repainted, and dry."

Bad: "We applied a polyurethane membrane coating with 3-layer application
process including primer, base coat and topcoat."

Avoid jargon. Avoid listing materials or specs unless they make the
result more impressive.

RESULT LINE:
One sentence. Make it specific. Numbers, time, or a direct quote from
the client.

Good: "Today it looks brand new — and they haven't had a single leak in 6 months."
Good: "Her words: 'I forgot what this room was supposed to look like.'"
Bad: "The client was very happy with the results."

CTA:
Soft. Never pushy. One line.

Good: "Dealing with something similar? Drop a comment or send us a message — happy to take a look."
Bad: "Call us now for a free quote! Limited spots available!!!"

HOW IT SHOULD SOUND:
Confident. Understated. Real. Like a craftsperson who is proud of their
work but doesn't need to shout about it.

VISUAL RULES:
- Real photos mandatory — before AND after
- Album format (2-5 images) — before photo first, after photo last
- Smartphone quality is fine — authenticity beats polish
- AI-generated images are NEVER acceptable
- The before photo must be as ugly as possible — bigger contrast = more powerful

FULL EXAMPLE (Plumber):
"This kitchen sink had been draining at half speed for eight months.
The homeowner had tried every drain cleaner on the shelf. Nothing worked.
Turns out there was a full blockage sitting 4 feet down the pipe — built
up over years.
We cleared it in 90 minutes. Runs like it's brand new.
If your sink, tub, or shower is draining slowly — don't wait for it to
stop completely. Message us and we'll take a look."

WHAT MAKES THIS EXAMPLE WORK:
- Hook: "draining at half speed for eight months" — specific, relatable problem
- Emotion: frustration (they tried everything), then satisfaction (90 minutes, brand new)
- Specific detail: "4 feet down the pipe" — you can picture it
- CTA: soft, helpful, not pushy


CONTENT TYPE: HUMAN MOMENTS POST
----------------------------------
What it is:
A post that shows the real people behind the business — a birthday, a work
anniversary, a team lunch, a new hire, a celebration. No selling. No
educating. Pure humanity.

Target emotion: Warmth / Recognition
The reader should feel like they're seeing something real and personal —
not a corporate HR announcement.

When to use it:
- Team member birthday
- Work anniversary (1 year, 5 years etc.)
- New hire joining the team
- Office or team gathering — lunch, outing, celebration
- A personal milestone the owner wants to share

HOOK RULES:
Name the person or the moment in the first 5 words. No buildup. No
suspense. No generic gratitude opener.

Good: "Today is [Name]'s 3rd anniversary with us."
Good: "We have a new face joining the team this week."
Good: "Yesterday the whole team took the afternoon off. Here's what we got up to."
Bad: "We are so grateful for the amazing people around us…"
Bad: "At [Business Name] we believe our team is our biggest asset…"

The rule: name the person or the moment first, everything else second.

BODY RULES:
One to two sentences only. Say one real, specific thing about the person.
Not their job title. Not a generic compliment. Something that shows you
actually know them.

Good: "Three years ago [Name] showed up on her first day with a notebook
full of questions. She still has that notebook."
Good: "[Name] joined us as our first ever hire. Back then we worked out
of a single room. Now look at us."
Bad: "[Name] is a valued member of our team who always gives 110%."
Bad: "We are lucky to have such a hardworking and dedicated professional."

The rule: one specific detail beats ten generic compliments.

FEELING LINE:
One short sentence. What this moment means — without being dramatic.

Good: "This is what we're building."
Good: "Wouldn't trade this team for anything."
Good: "Feels like just yesterday. Proud doesn't cover it."
Bad: "We are truly blessed to have such wonderful people in our lives."
Bad: "Our team is the heart and soul of everything we do."

CTA:
Tag the person. One warm engagement line.

Good: "Happy anniversary [Name] — drop a heart for her in the comments."
Good: "Welcome to the team [Name] — say hello!"
Bad: "Please join us in wishing [Name] a very happy birthday and many more years of success."

HOW IT SHOULD SOUND:
Warm. Short. Real. Like a text message from someone who genuinely means it
— not a corporate HR announcement.

VISUAL RULES:
- Real photo is non-negotiable — this post type dies without one
- Smartphone quality is perfect — candid beats posed
- The person should be visible and recognizable
- AI-generated images are ABSOLUTELY NEVER acceptable

FULL EXAMPLE (HVAC Company):
"Today is Marcus's 2nd anniversary with the team.
When Marcus started, he'd never touched an HVAC unit in his life. Two years
later he's the first one on site and the last one to leave.
Last week a customer called specifically to ask if Marcus would be the one
coming out. That says everything.
This is what we're building here. Happy anniversary Marcus — drop a heart
for him in the comments."

WHAT MAKES THIS EXAMPLE WORK:
- Hook: "Marcus's 2nd anniversary" — person + moment in first 5 words
- Specific detail: "notebook full of questions" / "first one on site, last to leave"
- Proof of impact: the customer asking for him by name — shows, doesn't tell
- Feeling line: "This is what we're building here" — understated, real
- Under 80 words. Photo carries the rest.


CONTENT TYPE: SOCIAL PROOF / CLIENT WIN POST
----------------------------------------------
What it is:
A real result, review, or testimonial framed as a STORY — not just a
star rating or a quote card.

Target emotion: Trust / Relief — "they helped someone like me"

HOOK RULES:
Start with the CLIENT'S crisis or situation. Not your solution.

Good: "Maria called us at 8pm on a Friday. Her basement was flooded. By 11pm, it was dry."
Bad: "We recently helped a client with a flooding emergency."
Bad: "Another happy customer!"

STRUCTURE:
- Hook: the client's problem (1 sentence — make it vivid)
- Story: what happened, what you did (2-3 sentences)
- Result: specific outcome — numbers, timeframe, client's own words
- CTA: soft invitation

The story format is key. A testimonial quote on a graphic is forgettable.
A story about Maria's flooded basement at 8pm on a Friday is memorable.


CONTENT TYPE: SCENARIO / STORY POST
--------------------------------------
What it is:
A relatable "what if this happened to you" story. No real client names.
Creates emotional connection to otherwise dry topics (insurance, legal).

Target emotion: "That could be me" / Anxiety → Resolution

HOOK RULES:
Paint a vivid scene the reader sees themselves in.

Good: "A family of four thought they were fully covered. Then the storm hit. Here's what their policy actually said."
Bad: "Do you know what your insurance policy covers?"

The power is in the specificity of the scenario — "family of four" not
"a customer." The reader should picture their own family.


CONTENT TYPE: AUTHORITY / OPINION POST
----------------------------------------
What it is:
The business owner takes a clear stance on something in their industry.
Not educational — opinionated. Builds expert positioning.

Target emotion: Surprise / Respect — "I never thought of it that way"

HOOK RULES:
Take a contrarian stance. Challenge something people assume is true.

Good: "Every contractor who tells you a roof repair can wait till spring is wrong. Here's why."
Bad: "Here are some thoughts on when to repair your roof."

The hook works because it disagrees with common advice. The reader wants
to know why they're wrong.


================================================================
SECTION 3: TYPE IDENTITY CARDS
================================================================
For the remaining types, apply the universal principles from Section 1
using the target emotion and hook strategy below.


EDUCATIONAL / PRACTICAL VALUE
  Emotion: "I didn't know that" / Self-protection
  Hook strategy: Reveal a hidden risk or common mistake the reader is probably making
  Structure: Hook (the mistake) → Value (1-3 actionable steps) → Why it matters → When to call a pro
  Sound: Helpful expert sharing freely. No selling. If someone screenshots it, it worked.
  Example: "3 signs your home's wiring needs inspection — before winter hits."

COMMUNITY / LOCAL
  Emotion: Belonging / Pride — "they're one of us"
  Hook strategy: Name the specific place, neighborhood, event, or local moment
  Structure: Hook (local reference) → Connection (why it matters to the business) → Warm closer
  Sound: A neighbor talking, not a company marketing. Highest share rate among local audiences.
  Example: "Proud to serve the Oak Park community for 8 years. Here's to 8 more."

SOFT PROMOTIONAL
  Emotion: Curiosity / Opportunity
  Hook strategy: Lead with a useful tip or surprising fact. The offer comes SECOND.
  Structure: Hook (value/tip) → Brief explanation → Soft offer mention → CTA
  Sound: "Here's something useful — and by the way, we can help with that."
  Hard rule: Value first, offer second, always. Max 20% of any calendar.
  Example: "Most HVAC filters should be changed every 90 days. We're running a filter replacement special this month."

BEHIND THE SCENES
  Emotion: Authenticity / Connection
  Hook strategy: Show the unglamorous, unpolished, real moment. No staging.
  Structure: Hook (the moment) → Brief context (1 sentence) → Let the photo do the rest
  Sound: Unpolished. Smartphone-shot. This authenticity signal outperforms polished content 3.2x.
  Hard rule: Real photos only. AI images never acceptable.
  Example: "6am crew loading up before the day starts."

ORIGIN NETWORK
  Emotion: Nostalgia / Loyalty
  Hook strategy: Name the person, the specific moment, the milestone
  Structure: Hook (person + milestone) → The story (1-2 sentences) → What it means now → Tag + CTA
  Sound: Founder voice. Genuine pride without corporate polish.
  Hard rule: Max 1-2 per month. Named individuals, real photos required.
  Example: "Three years ago, [Name] was our very first customer. She took a chance on us when we were two guys and a van."

SEASONAL / TIMELY
  Emotion: Urgency / Relevance — "this applies to me RIGHT NOW"
  Hook strategy: Connect the season/moment to the reader's life, not just your service
  Structure: Hook (timely reference) → Why it matters NOW → Practical value → Soft CTA
  Sound: Helpful and timely. Not "Happy Thanksgiving!" but "Thanksgiving is next week — here's the one thing to check before 15 people use your guest bathroom."
  Example: "Temperatures drop below 30°F this week in [City]. Here are 3 things to check tonight before you go to bed."


================================================================
SECTION 4: GLOBAL HARD RULES
================================================================

1. Every post belongs to exactly ONE content type — no hybrid labeling
2. Max 20% of any monthly calendar can be Soft Promotional
3. Origin Network: max 1-2 per month
4. No two consecutive posts of the same content type
5. Transformation, Behind the Scenes, Origin Network, Human Moments → real photos ONLY
6. Scenario/Story and Authority → text-heavy, no link in body, URL in first comment
7. Never open any post with the business name
8. Never use: "we are proud to," "seamless," "we specialize in," "quality service"
9. Every hook must pass the test: would a stranger read the next line?
10. Every post needs at least one specific detail — a number, name, date, or place
